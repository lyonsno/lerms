# Red Lerm Art Direction

This document captures the current visual thesis for red lerms before concept sheets, generated assets, sculpting, rigging, or final animation. It is an art-direction target, not an implementation contract and not proof that a body asset exists.

The gameplay spine remains in [campaign-map.md](campaign-map.md), and the first vertical data sockets remain in [first-vertical-interfaces.md](first-vertical-interfaces.md). This page answers a different question: what should a red lerm look like when it is simply being a red lerm?

## Core Thesis

Red lerms are eyeless, nose-led, squash-bodied thieves.

They behave as if they can see, but they should not have visible eyes. Attention, intelligence, appetite, and intent are expressed through the nose or nub, whole-body orientation, posture, arm reach, compression, and timing. A red lerm points its nub at whatever it wants.

The body should feel thick, ropey, and deformable: close to a worm-like body that can squash, stretch, lean, and revise its silhouette while moving. It should imply internal structure strongly enough to feel muscular or load-bearing, but never confirm bones in a hard anatomical sense.

Arms should read as real enough to steal and carry goins. Legs should be less explicit: lower-body pressure, underside pads, rear pseudopods, squashed contact points, or implied limb structure are preferred over clean humanoid legs.

## No Visible Eyes

No visible eyes is a load-bearing rule for the red-lerm direction.

Avoid:

- pupils, dots, glowing eyes, cartoon eyes, or hidden eye-slits;
- expressions that depend on eyebrows, eyelids, or gaze;
- standard cute-mascot face language.

Use instead:

- nose or nub orientation;
- head/body turn toward desire;
- arm reach and carry posture;
- compression, stretch, and recoil;
- whole-body anticipation and hesitation;
- sound and timing later, if audio enters the design.

The nub is not merely decoration. It is the attention pointer.

## Body Plan

A red lerm should sit between a simple procedural body and a sculptable character.

Useful ingredients:

- a squat worm/body mass;
- a blunt nose or nub;
- visible arms or arm-nubs capable of gripping/carrying;
- ambiguous lower body with implied legs or pressure pads;
- squash/stretch posture that can show running, lunging, tumbling, and recovering;
- enough thickness to feel like there may be ropey muscle under the surface;
- no confirmed hard skeleton.

The body should remain simple enough to swarm. Detail is useful only when it makes lerm appetite, theft, contact, or motion more readable.

## Reference Pressure

These are comparative reference pressures, not style ownership claims or exact targets.

- Mid/late-1990s through early-2000s stylized 3D game characters: literal, direct translation of odd proportions into 3D, with tactile or surprisingly material textures where useful.
- GameCube-era Nintendo character rendering: stylized proportions kept intact while surfaces can become more material, such as fabric-like or tactile treatment.
- Worms-like squash bodies: body shape changes continuously, with implied appendages and deformation carrying motion.
- Rayman-like semi-structured body weirdness: a body can feel limbed, animated, and readable without clean ordinary anatomy.
- Pikmin-like horde clarity: small bodies should be readable in groups and should have strong collective motion language.
- Lemming-like idiocy: group behavior can feel confident, stupid, and fatefully directed.

For audio later, red lerms should land somewhere between Pikmin-like squeaks and Worms-like squeaky chatter: more language-like than pure chirps, but not legible English.

## Cute And Awful

Red lerms must be able to look cute sometimes. They should also be able to look awful sometimes.

The target is not permanent cuteness and not permanent horror. The same body should support:

- eager thief energy;
- pathetic tumble or hit reaction;
- ridiculous goin obsession;
- small horde charm;
- occasional unpleasant appetite or body weirdness.

If a direction cannot become cute at all, it is too awful. If it cannot become awful at all, it is probably too mascot-clean.

## Open Axes For The Neutral Concept Sheet

The first concept sheet should explore neutral red lerms at rest or in a simple alert pose. It should not begin with the full steal/carry/flee/drop/reroute state matrix.

Useful axes:

- upright worm vs. low crawler;
- tiny nub vs. pronounced blunt nose;
- stub arms vs. grasping arms;
- smooth blob vs. ropey/folded body;
- implied feet vs. underside pads vs. rear pseudopods;
- cute-funny vs. uncanny-funny;
- procedural-feasible vs. sculpt/generator-needed;
- simple swarm body vs. more individual character body.

Every option on the first sheet should remain red, eyeless, nose-led, squash-bodied, and plausibly able to steal/carry goins.

## Do Not Explore Yet

Avoid these until a later design pass explicitly reopens them:

- visible eyes;
- normal humanoid legs;
- generic monster mouths;
- insect antennae or mandibles as the main attention cue;
- hard skeletal anatomy;
- armor, hats, clothing, or faction costumes;
- blue/yellow species design;
- final material variants;
- polished state animation before the neutral body direction is chosen.

## Production Route Is Separate From Identity

The visual target should be chosen before deciding whether the runtime body is procedural, generated, sculpted, rigged, or hybrid.

Possible production routes include:

- a procedural squash body;
- a simple authored mesh or rig;
- a generated mesh from a 2D concept;
- a hand sculpt or cleaned generated mesh;
- a hybrid where generated concepts inform an authored controllable body.

None of those routes should change the identity constraints above. A generated mesh that has visible eyes is not a valid red-lerm direction merely because it is high quality. A procedural body that lacks nose-led attention is not a valid red-lerm direction merely because it is easy to animate.

## Next Concept Gate

The next useful visual artifact is a neutral red-lerm identity sheet:

- 12-20 eyeless, nose-led red-lerm body thumbnails;
- same scale and comparable pose/view;
- no full behavior matrix yet;
- a spectrum from sphere-nub simplicity through procedural/squash feasibility and into sculpt/generator ambition;
- explicit notes on carry affordance, terrain contact, and body complexity.

After that sheet, pick a few finalists and only then make state/contact sheets showing approach, steal, carry, flee, hit, tumble, drop, and reroute.
