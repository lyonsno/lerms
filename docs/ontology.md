## The Underhill Account of LURMS, Glove Wealth, and Real Finger-Fluid Action

The neophyte is brought under the hill before they are shown the hill.

This is important. Surface-first introductions produce bad thinking. They see the glove, the treasure, the lerms, the juice, the bulbous slopes, and immediately begin saying terrible things like “tower defense” or “gesture game” or “cute physics battler.” These are not forbidden terms, exactly, but they are thin containers. They leak.

So the neophyte enters through the big round hatch beneath the hill of hills. The hatch is shut. The latch is suspect. Nobody pretends otherwise. It is a heavy door, not a perfect door. The mechanism has brass teeth, side bolts, one pressure gauge whose needle points to hmm, and a little listening tube that carries muffled crimes from above.

Inside Underhill: a kettle with no tea, a brass-and-wood espresso machine, a green-glass lerm terrarium, a goin tray, fungus lanterns, a blanket of unlicensed wool, a map that is always wrong, and several underpaid tradespeople pretending not to know each other.

This is where the world is studied. Not explained. Studied.

Above us is the Hill of Hills.

It is not a level. It is not terrain in the dead ordinary sense. It is a breathing manifold of bowls, ramps, ridges, divots, humps, gutters, lips, and little humiliating troughs. It rises and settles. It inverts slowly. A puddle that was a moat becomes a canal. A canal becomes a chute. A chute becomes a betrayal. The hill does not take sides, but it has opinions about balance and no respect for property.

At the top, or near the top, or wherever the hill has decided “top” means this minute, sits the glove.

Not a hand.

A glove.

This distinction is central. A hand grasps. A glove remembers grasping. A hand has bones and guilt and human jurisdiction. A glove is a false hand, a hand costume, a single lost half of a pair that woke up one day and concluded that all touched objects belong to it by ancient cuff law.

The glove has no arm. It does not acknowledge this as a defect.

It has a cuff, and the cuff is its house, lineage, ideology, and legal instrument. Velvet cuffs believe in hereditary treasure. Work cuffs believe in grime, weight, and earned custody. Rubber cuffs believe all fluids should be cataloged and all surfaces rinsed. Parade cuffs believe in ceremony, cleanliness, and losing wars.

The glove sits on glove wealth.

Glove wealth is not money. Money is merely one crude surface-world larval form of glove wealth. True glove wealth consists of small objects once touched, held, pocketed, lost, clasped, buttoned, flipped, worried, hidden, gripped, rubbed, or dropped by hands.

Coins. Rings. Keys. Buttons. Cufflinks. Thimbles. Bottle caps. Arcade tokens. Watch faces. Teeth in paper. Little charms. A brass washer with a mysterious authority. Anything that has absorbed enough hand-history can be claimed by glove law.

A glove does not believe it steals.

A glove believes it repatriates hand-adjacent matter.

The most important unit of glove wealth is the goin.

A goin begins as a coin, perhaps. But a coin exposed to glove custody, lerm saliva, hill damp, repeated wanting, and bad jurisprudence fattens. It loses denomination. Its face wears away. Its edge becomes ridged, dented, chewable. It becomes absurdly thick, no longer a token of exchange but a compact puck of portable desire.

When a goin rolls, it does not tinkle.

It goes:

chunk, chunk, chunk.

Or, on damp clay:

gunk, gunk, gunk.

A goin on the field is not an object. It is an event.

This brings us to the lerms.

The official cartridge spelling may say LURMS. The underhill field manuals say lerms, lurms, and occasionally something worse because the clerks are damp and no one trusts orthography around worm-lemming thieves. The neophyte should not become attached to spelling. The creature itself is prior to the word.

A lerm is something between a worm and a lemming, but that is only the taxonomic insult. More precisely: a lerm is an uphill appetite with a body. It is made of slope, greed, friction, and poor revision discipline.

A lerm does not have strategy. A lerm has vector.

Toward up.
Toward shiny.
Toward edible.
Away from certain juices.
Toward other lerms doing something stupid with confidence.

That is enough.

There are three ordinary lerm species, though “ordinary” is a courtesy extended by underhill scholars who should know better.

Red lerms are impulse criminals. They are short, springy, overeager, and almost offensively committed to forwardness. If a goin drops nearby, a red lerm does not decide to chase it; the red lerm becomes the chase. It leaps badly. It overshoots. It steals with enthusiasm and carries with no technique. Red lerms make history happen in the stupidest available direction.

Blue lerms are substrate believers. Long, low, slick, slow, infrastructural. They do not merely cross terrain; they negotiate with it using their whole damp length. They carry heavy things. They build. They make goin berms, goin ramps, coin gutters, jewel cradles, tunnel plugs, coffee-ground retaining walls if given access to espresso waste. A blue lerm is how theft becomes architecture.

Yellow lerms are corruption in motion. Twitchy, opportunistic, responsive to false shinies, reflections, dropped buttons, little glints on glass, rumor fields, and the visible excitement of other yellow lerms. They are the reason no battlefield remains honest. They defect from the main assault to chase decoys, then defect from the decoys to chase a loose ruby, then accidentally discover a better path than everyone else. A yellow lerm is not smart. It is beautifully vulnerable to the wrong stimulus.

The lerms do not simply steal glove wealth and leave the game poorer.

They metabolize it.

Goins become structures: bridges, berms, hatchery roofs, sluices, ramps, heat plates for lermlets, bad masonry with civic implications.

Jewels become growths: ruby brambles, sapphire gills, emerald lure-bulbs, mineral fungus, glowing root systems, species pressure.

Buttons become beacons. Rings become gates. Thimbles become bunkers. Cufflinks become little civic disasters.

If a lerm steals a goin, the hoard loses warmth. If enough warmth leaves the hoard, the glove’s cuff bell rings and the glove wakes into aristocratic insult.

If a lerm drops a goin, nearby lerms may break formation to chase it. This is not a bug. This is lerm politics. A loose goin is an election. A rolling ruby is a coup. A false button is a constitutional crisis for yellow lerms.

The player, meanwhile, is the glove.

Or, in two-player forms, one of several rival gloves, each attempting to bias a third-party criminal ecosystem into stealing the other glove’s wealth. Nobody owns the lerms. The lerms are not units. They are opportunistic terrain vermin with economic consequences. One does not command lerms. One corrupts their vector field.

And because the glove cannot touch, the glove secretes.

This is the whole hinge of the world.

The glove is a false hand in a world where grasping has been abolished. Contact is exiled. Touch is replaced by finger juice.

The promotional box may say REAL FINGER-FLUID ACTION because some coward in legal thought “finger juice” sounded actionable. But underhill uses the true vernacular. Finger juice is what comes from the pores at the ends of the glove’s fingers. Not beams. Not spells. Not elegant magic. Pores.

Soft fabric glands. Puckered little nozzles. Each fingertip dimples, swells, and emits.

Index finger: sharp blue knockback juice. It hits red lerms and sends them tumbling into their own convictions.

Middle finger: white adhesive gunk. Yes, everyone noticed. It gums terrain, slows lerms, catches goins, and may accidentally provide blue lerms with construction material if left alone too long. The middle finger has acquired legal counsel.

Ring finger: green fertilizer juice. It grows moss, vines, fungus pads, and obstruction flora. It also feeds lerms if they eat it. This is typical glove policy: every solution contains breakfast for the enemy.

Thumb: amber slick. Heavy, warm, goin-moving, gutter-making. It can turn a bowl into a chute and a chute into a theft corridor.

Pinky: purple weirdness. Nobody responsibly tested it. Yellow lerms love it, which is the first sign of danger.

Finger juice is not merely damage. It is weather, force, incentive, terrain modification, growth trigger, goin transport, and glove personality rendered as fluid. Every finger is a way for a false hand to act without touching. The gameplay is not “shoot enemies.” It is secretion-based ecology management under theft pressure.

The underhill trades exist because this ecology is too strange to be left to official institutions.

There are goin fatteners who warm and dampen ordinary coins until they become thick enough to matter.

Pore-cap polishers who maintain little brass and silver caps for ceremonial or tactical fingertip suppression.

Cuff menders who preserve glove identity through cloth repair and therefore practice politics with needles.

Lerm drovers who do not herd lerms, because no one herds lerms, but can bias them using sounds, false shinies, and very specific lies.

Fungus whisperers who deny whispering to fungus while knowing exactly which spores prefer hoard warmth.

Slope listeners who press their ears to roots and predict which bowl is about to become a chute.

Retrievers who go aboveground after storms and battles to recover loose goins, cracked jewels, spent pore caps, and whatever the lerms have not fully digested into civilization.

Blank-form clerks who maintain wet ledgers that mildew into nonsense and call it administration.

And in the side chamber, because Underhill is no longer merely shelter but an actual illicit civic substrate, there is the lerm terrarium.

A small green-glass tank with damp clay, one miniature ridge, one bowl, three visible goins, and at least two more lerms than the records admit. It is officially for research. In practice it is a sleep lamp, small criminal aquarium, behavior test harness, and proof that lerms need very little room to invent larceny.

A red lerm launches into the glass.
A blue lerm rearranges the hill overnight.
A yellow lerm falls in love with condensation.
A hidden lermlet warms under a goin and develops early-stage theft impulses.

The terrarium rustles while the kettle ticks.

Underhill itself is not the game’s explanation. It is the frame through which one learns to see the game correctly.

Above: glove wealth, hill breath, lerm thievery, goin rolls, finger juice, fluid laws, crime weather.

Below: kettle with no tea, espresso machine of brass and wood, fungus lanterns, broken latch, listening tube, trades, terrarium, records of things too damp to write.

The neophyte eventually understands that LURMS is not about defending treasure in the ordinary way. It is about a glove guarding a hoard it cannot hold, using fluids it secretes from pores, against worm-lemming thieves that convert stolen wealth into terrain, offspring, infrastructure, and political movement.

It is about property becoming ecology.

It is about a false hand replacing touch with weather.

It is about thick coins rolling through a world where greed has physics.

It is about the hill itself remembering every bad decision and slowly tilting them into new forms.

And somewhere beneath all this, in the warm green-dark of Underhill, the kettle remains on. The latch remains imperfect. The espresso machine groans. The lerm terrarium revises its population count. The listening tube carries a distant gunk, gunk, gunk from above.

A goin is live on the field.

The glove has noticed.

The lerms are already moving.
